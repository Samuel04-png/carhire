S E C T I O N  1
The Problem With AI-Generated Copy
When AI tools build websites without proper copy direction, they produce text that sounds like it 
is describing the website rather than speaking to the client. It explains what the design does 
instead of what the business offers. It references the interface instead of the outcome. It uses 
meta-language — words that only make sense if you know a website is being built.
This is not copy. This is scaffolding. It has to be removed and replaced before the site goes live.
1.1  Examples of Broken AI Copy (The Before)
Real examples found on this site that must be removed or replaced:
  'Why this rebuild focuses on both image and operations.'❌
Problem: 'Rebuild' is internal language. Clients don't know or care this is a redesign. This sentence 
belongs in a developer brief, not on a live website.
  'The original project had the right intent but not enough connected logic.'❌
Problem: 'The original project' is visible to clients on the About page. This exposes internal thinking, 
sounds unprofessional, and destroys trust.
  'Vehicle details, booking totals, account history, and admin status views now relate to each ❌
other through the same state model.'
Problem: 'State model' is developer jargon. A client booking a car for their wedding does not need to 
know how the database is structured.
  'These value cards are intentionally more substantial than generic marketing copy because ❌
the page needs to build trust for high-value bookings.'
Problem: This is the AI explaining its own design decisions to itself, on a live public page. It should 
never be visible to any client.
  '1 Shared source of truth for public and admin views'❌
Problem: This is a technical architecture note presented as a client-facing brand stat. Clients care 
about their experience, not the codebase.
1.2  Why This Happens
AI tools generate copy based on the context they are given. When the context is a build 
specification or a design brief, the AI writes copy that sounds like it is narrating the spec. It says 
things like 'this section is designed to build trust' instead of actually building trust through the 
words it uses.
The fix is not to ask the AI to 'be more professional.' The fix is to give the AI a clear brand voice, 
a clear audience, a list of banned phrases, and concrete examples of what good copy sounds 
like in this sector. That is exactly what this document provides.

2.3  Banned Words & Phrases
The following words and phrases must never appear in client-facing copy on the Shark Car Hire 
website. They are either too generic, too technical, too vague, or too AI-typical to belong on a 
premium brand's website.
Never use these words or phrases in client-facing copy:
Technical / Build Language (belongs in specs, not on the site):
• rebuild, redesign, original project, previous version, state model, data model, 
connected logic, frontend, backend, component, module, integrated system
Generic Marketing Filler (says nothing, trusted by no one):
• world-class, best-in-class, cutting-edge, next-level, seamless, comprehensive, 
holistic, leverage, synergy, innovative, solutions, empower, transform
Hedge Words (undermine confidence):
• we try to, we aim to, we hope to, we strive to, we endeavour to, we believe, we think, 
we feel, arguably, potentially, in some cases

AI Meta-Language (the AI narrating itself):
• this section, this page, this card, this component, intentionally, designed to, built to 
build trust, the user, the visitor, the interface, the experience is designed
Vague Claims (unverifiable, unconvincing):
• all your needs, various options, excellent service, high quality, top-notch, premier, 
superior, outstanding, exceptional
Preferred words and phrases for this brand:
• punctual, confirmed, serviced, insured, assigned, professional, trusted, verified, 
ready, direct, clear
• journey, arrival, departure, transfer, route, collection, handover, fleet, vehicle, 
booking, itinerary
• executive, corporate, premium, reliable, consistent, precise, on-time, prepared
• Lusaka, Kitwe, Ndola, Zambia, airport, city centre, hotel, office — always name the 
place specifically
• your driver, your vehicle, your booking, your journey — personal and specific
• instantly confirmed, same-day, 24-hour, morning of your trip, the evening before — 
time-specific language

S E C T I O N  3
The Master AI Rewrite Prompt
Copy this entire prompt and paste it into Claude, ChatGPT-4o, or any capable AI writing tool. 
Then follow it with the copy you want audited and rewritten. The AI will follow these instructions 
to produce client-ready copy.
You can use this prompt in two modes: AUDIT mode (the AI tells you what is wrong) or 
REWRITE mode (the AI rewrites the copy directly). Both modes are included below.
3.1  Full Prompt — Paste This Into Any AI Tool
── SYSTEM INSTRUCTIONS ──
You are a senior brand copywriter specialising in premium automotive and
executive mobility services. You have written copy for car hire companies,
chauffeur services, airport transfer businesses, and corporate transport
operators across Africa and internationally.
The client is Shark Car Hire Limited — a premium car hire company operating
across Lusaka, Kitwe, and Ndola, Zambia. Services include airport transfers,
corporate hire, wedding cars, event vehicles, self-drive hire, and long-term
lease. Their clients include corporate executives, NGO professionals, government
officials, event planners, and individual travellers who value reliability.
── BRAND VOICE ──
Tone: Confident, premium, human, locally proud, precise.
Voice: A knowledgeable professional who runs a serious business and respects
their clients' time and intelligence.
── WHAT YOU MUST NEVER WRITE ──
1. Never use technical or build language: rebuild, redesign, state model,
   data model, connected logic, frontend, backend, component, module.
2. Never narrate the interface: 'this section', 'this page', 'this card',
   'designed to build trust', 'intentionally more substantial'.
3. Never use generic filler: world-class, cutting-edge, seamless,
   innovative, holistic, leverage, synergy, comprehensive.
4. Never hedge: 'we try to', 'we aim to', 'we hope to', 'we strive to'.
5. Never make vague claims: 'excellent service', 'all your needs',
   'high quality', 'various options', 'superior experience'.
── WHAT YOU MUST ALWAYS DO ──
1. Speak directly to the client — use 'you' and 'your' naturally.
2. Be specific — name the city, the service, the outcome, the timeframe.
3. State facts confidently — 'Your driver is confirmed the evening before
   your booking.' Not 'We will try to inform you of your driver details.'
4. Match the energy to the context — high for headlines, calm for process
   steps, warm for confirmation pages.

--- PAGE 7 ---
5. Keep sentences short. One idea per sentence. Active voice always.
6. Write for a client who is time-poor, discerning, and has options.
7. Every paragraph should make the client want to take the next step.
── YOUR TASK ──
I will give you the current copy from a specific page of the Shark Car Hire
website. You will:
STEP 1 — AUDIT: List every sentence or phrase that violates the rules
above. For each violation, state which rule it breaks and why.
STEP 2 — REWRITE: Rewrite every piece of copy on this page from scratch.
Use the brand voice, the preferred language, and the context for this
specific page type. Match the structure of the original (same headings,
same sections, same stat blocks) but replace all the words.
STEP 3 — STATS CHECK: Flag any placeholder stats (28+, 1 shared source
of truth, etc.) and suggest what the real, client-meaningful stat
should be once confirmed with the client.
Do not explain what you are doing. Do not add commentary. Just deliver
the audit findings and the rewritten copy, clearly separated.

Page-by-Page Rewrite Guide
Use this section as a reference when running the AI prompt above on each specific page. It tells 
the AI the exact context, goal, and any special instructions for each page type. Add this 
guidance after the main prompt when rewriting a specific page.
4.1  Homepage
Add to prompt when rewriting the Homepage:
This is the homepage. The client lands here from a Google search, a social media post, or a 
referral. They decide within 5 seconds whether this business is worth their time. The headline 
must arrest them. The subheadline must confirm they are in the right place. The CTA must be 
unmissable. Do not try to say everything — say the most important thing perfectly. The hero 
copy is the most valuable real estate on the site. Every word must earn its position.
Stats section: Replace placeholder stats with these — confirm actual numbers with client 
first:
• [X] Vehicles — ask Shark Car Hire for actual fleet count
• [X] Years in Operation — ask for founding year
• [X]+ Completed Bookings — ask for cumulative bookings
• 3 Cities — Lusaka · Kitwe · Ndola (this one is confirmed)
4.2  About Us Page
Add to prompt when rewriting the About Us Page:
This is the About page. The client is here because they want to know who is behind the 
business before they trust them with their executive travel or their wedding. The copy must tell a 
real story about Shark Car Hire — why it was founded, what drives it, and what it stands for. 
Avoid generic mission statements. Every statement must be specific enough to be true only of 
Shark Car Hire, not any other car hire company. Remove every trace of design commentary, 
build language, or placeholder copy. The values section must read like convictions, not 
marketing claims.
Specific instructions for the Values section:
• Each value must be named with one strong noun or short phrase — not a sentence
• The description must be 1-2 sentences maximum — precise, not elaborate
• Values must be specific to this business, not generic to all businesses
• Remove all commentary about 'why these values were written this way'
Suggested replacement values to consider (confirm with client):

• Prepared Before You Ask — Your driver knows your flight number. Your vehicle is

4.3  Fleet Page
Add to prompt when rewriting the Fleet Page:
This is the fleet page. The client is actively looking for a vehicle. The copy must help them find 
what they need quickly and give them confidence in what they are choosing. Vehicle 
descriptions must be specific — not 'comfortable and spacious' but 'seats 7, full climate control, 
ideal for family airport transfers or group corporate travel.' Every vehicle description should tell 
the client exactly who this vehicle is for and why they should choose it. Avoid repeating the 
same adjectives across vehicles.
Vehicle description formula to follow:
• Line 1: What the vehicle is (make, model, category)
• Line 2: Who it is best suited for (corporate solo travel, family groups, wedding parties)
• Line 3: What makes it stand out (specific feature, comfort level, capacity)
• Line 4: What service types it is available for (self-drive, chauffeur, or both)
4.4  Service Pages
Add to prompt when rewriting any Service Page:
This is a service page. The client has clicked because they have a specific need — an airport 
transfer, a corporate account, a wedding car. They are not browsing. They are deciding. The 
copy must speak directly to that specific need. Open with the client's situation, not the 
company's offering. Show that Shark Car Hire understands exactly what this service involves 
and what can go wrong if they choose the wrong provider. Then show why Shark Car Hire is 
the right choice — specifically, not generally.
Service page opening formulas by service type:
• Airport Transfers: Open with the stress of missing a flight or waiting for a driver who 
doesn't show.
• Corporate Hire: Open with the stakes — an executive's time is not replaceable.
• Weddings: Open with the emotional weight — this day happens once.
• Events: Open with the logistics challenge — moving multiple people, on time, without

• Self-Drive: Open with the freedom — your route, your schedule, your pace.
• Long Term: Open with the cost and hassle of ownership versus the simplicity of a

4.5  Booking Flow Copy
Add to prompt when rewriting Booking Flow copy:
This is the booking flow. The client has decided to book. Do not let copy get in the way. Every 
instruction must be one sentence. Every label must be self-explanatory. Every error message 
must tell the client exactly what to fix, not just that something is wrong. Step titles must be 
action-oriented. Confirmation messages must be warm and specific. The client should feel 
guided, not processed.
Booking flow copy rules:
• Step titles: 'Your Trip Details' not 'Step 1'. 'Your Extras' not 'Add-Ons'. 'Your Details' 
not 'Customer Information'. 'Review & Confirm' not 'Step 4'.
• Button copy: 'Continue to Extras' not 'Next'. 'Confirm My Booking' not 'Submit'. 'Go 
Back' not 'Previous'.
• Error messages: 'Return date must be after your pickup date.' not 'Invalid date range.'
• Empty states: 'No vehicles available on these dates in Ndola. Call us — we may have 
options.' not 'No results found.'
• Confirmation: 'Your booking is confirmed. Reference: SCH-[REF]. Your driver will 
contact you the evening before your pickup.' not 'Booking successful.'
4.6  Admin Dashboard Copy
Add to prompt when rewriting Admin Dashboard copy:
This is the admin dashboard. The audience is Shark Car Hire's internal team — booking 
agents, operations managers, drivers, and directors. The copy here is functional, not 
persuasive. It should be direct, scannable, and unambiguous. No marketing language. No 
flowery descriptions. Labels should be as short as possible while remaining clear. Status 
messages should tell the user exactly what action is required. Empty states should guide the 
user toward the correct next step.
Admin copy rules:
• Dashboard section headers: 'Today's Bookings' not 'Booking Overview'. 'Vehicles On 
Road' not 'Active Fleet Status'.
• Alert messages: 'Payment overdue — 3 bookings' not 'There are some bookings that 
may require attention.'
• Empty states: 'No bookings today. Check tomorrow's schedule.' not 'No data to

• Confirmation dialogs: 'Cancel this booking? The client will be notified and the vehicle
